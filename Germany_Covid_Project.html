Introduction & Research Questions

The 2019 Novel Coronavirus (COVID-19) continues to spread in countries around the world and we cannot see the end of this pandemic. While some countries remove all the restrictions such as wearing masks in public places, prohibition on public gatherings etc., Germany has arguably remained the only European country where people must wear a FFP2 mask in public transport. The recent news on COVID-19 spread in Germany has been quite concerning, as the number of cases started to rise again. So, we decided to dive deep in the coronavirus outbreak in Germany and analyse the dynamics of cases and deaths in the country, find out the most affected German States as well as the most affected age groups among Germany’s population, compare the infection rate between males and females, determine the most popular vaccine for German citizens and understand whether the targeted vaccination rate stated by the German government was achieved. For all these research questions to be answered we used R programming language and its versatile powerful packages.

Content

The dataset we have used was obtained from Kaggle. It consists of three main csv files:

  1. covid_de.csv: COVID-19 cases and deaths which are updated daily. The original data are being collected by Germany’s Robert Koch Institute and can be download through the National Platform for Geographic Data
  2. demographics_de.csv: General Demographic Data about Germany on the federal state level. This data has been downloaded from Germany’s Federal Office for Statistics (Statistisches Bundesamt) through their Open Data platform GENESIS.
  3. covid_de_vaccines.csv: COVID-19 vaccination progress for Germany as a whole. This dataset details daily doses, broken down cumulatively by manufacturer, as well as the cumulative number of people having received their first and full vaccination.

Let us start with the setup of the working space in R.

Setup

We need to get rid of any possible data in our cache and load all the libraries which are useful for our data analysis. We will use the tidyverse package to get access to a great variety of packages such as dplyr and ggplot2 which helps us to clean and visualize the data. The package leaflet helps us create an interactive clustered map for a better coronavirus cases representation. RColorBrewer allows us to pick certain color gradients and is an extremely helpful and underrated tool for EDA. Package shiny is used to create a shiny markdown in order to have a better visualization of our final report. Finally, plotly helps us create interactive web-based graphs.

Vaccine Analysis


Here we are going to explore our third dataset,
covid_de_vaccines, to analyse the speed of vaccination by
each of three vaccines. Unfortunately, the data given is limited to the
cumulative number of vaccines and date of first and second doses, so we
were not able to join the dataset with the previous ones. However, it is
still interesting to know which vaccine was known to be the most popular
one and how the trend has changed.





From the line graph we may clearly see the popularity of Pfizer
vaccine, which more than 130 million people received by June 2022. Only
around 30 million people were vaccinated with Moderna, which is followed
by nearly 13 million getting Astra Zeneca vaccine. Notably, we may
observe a speed curve of Pfizer vaccination starting from February 2021.
From our perspective, such a high popularity of Pfizer may be explained
by the decision of German government make a Pfizer vaccine a default
option and therefore German citizens simply did not have a choice to
choose the vaccine producer. Moreover, after some time since launching
Astra Zeneca was known to have many side-effects and therefore the usage
of this vaccine type was decreased dramatically.






Targeted vaccination rate


Now we want to check if the target vaccination rate of 90% stated by
the German government was achieved. At first let’s calculate total
German population.


## [1] 83019213


We can now create a bar gauge for the current vaccination rate in
Germany, namely the relative number of people who got the first jab and
the percent of citizens who received a full vaccination - 2 doses of

From these graphs we may conclude that 77.6% of the German population
received one dose of the vaccine and 76% are fully vaccinated. However,
the target of 90% set by the German government was not yet achieved.
Thus, at the moment almost every fourth person has not received a jab
against COVID-19, so German population has not developed a herd immunity
yet and therefore the number of coronavirus cases started to rise again
this summer.








Conclusion


After a detailed exploratory data analysis of our dataset we have
come to the following conclusions:


    

  1. The most COVID-19 affected German States turned out to be the most
populated districts - Nordrhein-Westfallen, Bayern and
Baden-Wuerttemberg. Although Sachsen has a much lower population and
number of recorded cases than Baden-Wuerttemberg its death toll appeared
to be almost the same as in this State, most likely because of the
ageing population.
    2. 

  2. The most infected age group is the largest one - people aged from 35
to 59 years old, while the highest mortality rate is observed among the
elderly people - 80-99. So, the older a person is the higher the
likelihood that this person dies from coronavirus.
    3. 

  3. The proportion of females that caught COVID-19 is few percents
higher, since the number of women living in Germany is higher than that
of men; however, roughly 7% more males died from coronavirus.
    4. 

  4. Among 3 different vaccine producers, the number of people that
received Pfizer jab is significantly higher than that of Moderna and
Astra Zeneca and makes up more than 130 million doses administered by
June 10, 2022. It is also notable, that Astra Zeneca vaccines were
discontinued in July 2021 due to discovered rare blood clots.
    5. 

  5. The targeted vaccination rate of 90% stated by the German government
was not yet achieved. Current figures stand at 76% for fully vaccinated
German population, which means that almost every fourth person in
Germany has still not received a jab. That is why the number of
coronavirus cases started to surge again this summer.



    6.
